If someone you love is thinking about ibogaine, your first question is probably not "how does it work?" It's "could this hurt them?"

That's the right question to ask, and you deserve a straight answer. Ibogaine has helped many people interrupt addictions that nothing else could touch. It also carries real, serious risks that should never be brushed aside. Both things are true, and understanding them is how you help a loved one make a safe decision.

## Why ibogaine carries risk

Ibogaine's most serious danger is to the **heart**. It can affect the heart's electrical rhythm, specifically by prolonging what's called the QT interval, which in some people can trigger a dangerous, even fatal, arrhythmia.

The deaths that have been associated with ibogaine are most often linked to a few specific, avoidable factors:

- **Undiagnosed heart conditions:** someone with a pre-existing cardiac problem they didn't know about.
- **Dangerous drug interactions:** other substances or medications in the body that shouldn't be mixed with ibogaine.
- **No medical supervision:** taking it at home, or at an unqualified operation with no screening and no one able to respond to an emergency.

The pattern is important: the greatest danger comes from ibogaine taken *without proper screening and supervision.* That's the part families can actually influence.

## What makes it safer

A responsible ibogaine provider treats it as the serious medical procedure it is. Before your loved one goes, here is what safety actually looks like:

- **Thorough screening:** a full medical history, an EKG to check heart rhythm, bloodwork, and a review of every medication and substance the person uses. Nothing hidden.
- **Continuous monitoring:** heart monitoring throughout the session, not just a check at the start.
- **Trained staff and emergency equipment on site:** people who can recognize and respond to a cardiac event immediately.
- **An honest, experienced provider:** one that turns people away when it isn't safe for them, rather than accepting everyone who can pay.

> A provider willing to skip the screening is telling you something important about how they'll treat your loved one's life.

## Questions to ask before a loved one goes

You don't need to be a doctor to help vet a provider. These questions reveal a great deal:

- Will there be a full cardiac screening and EKG beforehand?
- Who is medically present during the session, and what are their qualifications?
- What emergency equipment and protocols are on site?
- How do you decide when *not* to treat someone?
- What medications or conditions would rule my loved one out?

An honest provider will welcome these questions. If they're evasive, that's an answer in itself.
